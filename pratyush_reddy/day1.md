OWASP IoT Top 10: Quick Summaries
1. Weak, Guessable, or Hardcoded Passwords: Devices are often shipped with easily guessable or unchangeable default credentials that attackers can quickly exploit to gain unauthorized access.
2.Insecure Network Services: Leaving unnecessary network ports open and exposed to the internet provides attackers with easy entry points to compromise the device.
3.Insecure Ecosystem Interfaces: Weaknesses in the companion mobile apps, cloud APIs, or web dashboards can allow attackers to completely bypass device-level security.
4. Lack of Secure Update Mechanism: Without encrypted and verified firmware updates, devices cannot safely receive critical security patches, leaving them permanently vulnerable.
5. Use of Insecure or Outdated Components: Relying on deprecated or vulnerable third-party software libraries introduces deep supply chain risks into the device's core architecture.
6. Insufficient Privacy Protection: Devices often collect and transmit sensitive personal data without proper safeguards or explicit user consent, leading to severe privacy violations.
7. Insecure Data Transfer and Storage: Failing to encrypt sensitive data both while it is stored on the device and as it travels across the network makes it easy for attackers to intercept.
8.Lack of Device Management: Without tools for remote monitoring, patching, and secure decommissioning, widespread deployments of IoT devices become nearly impossible to secure over time.
9. Insecure Default Settings: Shipping devices with unsafe configurations out-of-the-box puts the burden on end-users to manually secure them, which rarely happens.
10. Lack of Physical Hardening: Attackers with physical access to a device can easily extract encryption keys or manipulate firmware if the hardware itself lacks anti-tamper protections.
